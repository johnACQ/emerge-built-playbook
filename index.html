01

The opportunity

The wind is at your back harder than it has ever been. Three things are happening at the same time: a housing shortage nobody can build their way out of by hand, a wave of law changes that made a second home legal on nearly every lot out west, and cheap government money to pay for it. Factory-built is the only answer that scales. That's you.

The math doesn't work without you

Canada has to roughly double its home-building pace, and CMHC says outright it can't be done "without a significantly larger and modernized workforce."1 But nearly one in four tradespeople in Alberta and BC walks off the site permanently within about seven years.3 You can't hammer your way out of this. The only lever that bends the curve is moving the labour indoors, into a factory, which is exactly what your 40-plus patents and your one-floor-of-units-a-day crane process already do.

The law just handed you a market

BC's Bill 44 killed single-family-only zoning province-wide. Three to four units are now legal as-of-right on essentially every residential lot, up to six near transit.5 Edmonton allows up to eight units on most low-density lots.6 Overnight, hundreds of thousands of ordinary backyards became legal build sites. Most of the homeowners who own them still have no idea. That gap between "it's legal now" and "nobody told me" is the whole opportunity.

Straight talk

This isn't a case for advertising your whole business. Your developer and multi-family capacity sells through relationships, procurement and Greg's network, not a Facebook ad, and I won't pretend otherwise. The residential line is the piece that paid acquisition wins, and it's the fastest cash in the building. That's where this playbook points. The bigger B2B pipeline is Phase 2, and I've laid out how we feed it too.

02

The wedge

The residential modular line is where we start, for three reasons: the buyer is reachable with ads, the regulation change is a timer that creates urgency for us, and every competitor in the market is asleep on the exact things that convert.

Reachable

They're online and they're searching

Vancouver alone has built 7,000-plus laneway homes since 2009.10 The homeowner researching a backyard suite Googles "cost to build a laneway house" and scrolls Instagram at night. Unlike your developer buyers, this one can be found and sold with paid media.

Urgent

The clock is real, not manufactured

Calgary's blanket rezoning is set to be repealed on Aug 4, 2026, but applications filed before that date are grandfathered.7 Calgary's suite incentive went to a waitlist in June 2026.8 These are genuine "build now or miss it" levers we can point ads at without inventing anything.

Uncontested

The field left the door open

The modular and ADU builders out west hide their pricing, offer no financing help, and never tell the rental-income story. The two brands that do the opposite are the two that are winning.11 We copy what works and out-execute everyone who's coasting.

03

The buyer

We're not selling to "homeowners." We're selling to four specific people, each with a different reason to build, each needing a different ad. Getting this right is 80% of why the campaign works. Here's who they are and what actually moves them.

1. The caregiver

"I want Mom close, but not under the same roof." Multigenerational households are the fastest-growing household type in the country, up 21% since 2001.9 This buyer is emotional, not price-shopping. Sell dignity, independence, and peace of mind. A suite in the backyard beats a care home.

2. The parent of an adult kid

"My son can't afford to move out." Roughly 2.5 million Canadians over 25 still live with their parents.9 This buyer wants their kid launched and their home worth more. Sell a place for them to live now that becomes a rental later.

3. The income investor

"I want the backyard to pay me." A Vancouver laneway can rent for $2,000 to $3,200 a month, and a legal suite typically lifts the property value by a large share of what it cost to build.12 Sell the payback math and the equity, hard numbers, calculator on the page.

4. The aspiring small developer

"Bill 44 says I can put four units here." Owns a lot, sees the new zoning, wants to build but has never done it. Sell certainty: fixed factory price, one crane day, no trade-shortage delays. This buyer can also become a reseller in their town, which feeds Phase 2.

Where Alberta differs

Albertans don't type "laneway." They type "backyard suite," "garage suite," "garden suite." We buy every local synonym so we're not paying for the wrong province's language. And the cheap-money angle is huge here: Ottawa now lends up to $80,000 at 2% over 15 years to build a suite.8 For most of these four people, "how do I pay for it" is the real objection, and almost nobody in the market is answering it.

04

The landscape

I pulled apart how every modular and ADU builder out west markets themselves. The pattern is clear: they compete on product and neglect the funnel. Here's the field, and the seven open doors we walk through.

Who's out there

  • ROC Modular (S. Alberta) - your closest peer. Markets like a contractor: no pricing, thin galleries, "Contact Us." Beatable on the funnel.
  • Honomobo (Edmonton) - the best-marketed player and the one to study. Shows prices openly, has a financing page, strong PR. This is the bar.11
  • Smallworks / Lanefab (Vancouver) - own the "laneway cost" content and SEO by publishing full price breakdowns.
  • Modworks (Edmonton/Calgary) - closest AB ADU peer, and asleep: no pricing, no financing, no ROI story.
  • Stack Modular - manufactures in Shanghai. Wide open to a "built in Alberta, no ocean freight, no tariff risk" attack.
  • NRB / ATCO - the scale incumbent, but procurement-focused with no consumer funnel at all.

The seven gaps we exploit

  • Pricing opacity - everyone hides it; the two who publish, win. We publish a "starting from" and a calculator.
  • No financing guidance - we own the $80K federal loan / HELOC / refi conversation.
  • No rental-income story - we make the suite pay for itself on the page, with real payback math.
  • Decayed local SEO - "garage suite builder Edmonton," "modular apartment Alberta" are under-defended.
  • "Built in Alberta" is unclaimed - local steel, local jobs, no offshore risk. You own it.
  • No B2B lead magnets - a developer "is your site modular-ready?" report captures pipeline nobody else asks for.
  • No configurators - a pick-a-model, get-an-estimate tool would lead the category.

The takeaway: the winners in this market win on transparency, content and financing, not on a better box. You already have the best box, plus a genuine "27 years, 40-plus patents, one floor a day, CSA-approved so the city doesn't re-inspect" story that none of them can match. We just have to say it louder and put a funnel under it.

05

M1 · The message

This is the first of the three Ms every engine runs on: Message, Marketing, Method. Message is the words and the offer. Get this wrong and no amount of ad spend saves you. You said it yourself on our call: sell the outcome, not the factory. Nobody buys structural steel. They buy their mother living next door.

The core reposition

Away from "faster, greener, quieter construction" (true, but it's about you) and toward the outcome the buyer pictures: "A second home in your backyard. Built in our factory, craned in, livable in weeks not years." We keep your proof points (27 years, 40-plus patents, CSA-approved, half the time of traditional build) as the reasons-to-believe underneath the promise, never as the headline.

The offer that converts

A high-consideration $200K-plus decision stalls on uncertainty. We kill it with a concrete, low-friction first step: "Find out what your lot qualifies for and what your suite would cost, free, in 3 minutes." That's the lead magnet, the ad promise, and the landing-page headline, all matched. No "request a consultation." A specific, valuable, instant answer.

The one-page story

Your backyard is now legal to build on → most people don't know → here's what yours qualifies for → here's the suite → here's what it rents for and what it adds to your home → here's how to pay for it → book a call. Every ad, every page, every email tells one slice of that story. That's the Rule of One in action: one reader, one promise, one next step.

M2 · The marketing

The second M: how we get in front of the right people and make them raise a hand. Three channels, each with a job. We buy the demand that already exists, manufacture the demand that doesn't, and borrow trust from people who already have it.

1. Google Search + Map Pack

Capture intent

The buyer who types "garage suite builder Calgary" is ready now. We own those searches and the local map pack. This is the lowest-funnel, highest-return dollar, and the field has half-abandoned it.10

Budget to start: $60-100/day, tight exact and phrase match, hard negative-keyword wall so we never pay for tire-kickers or the wrong province's terms.

2. Meta (Instagram + Facebook)

Create demand

Most homeowners don't know their lot is now legal to build on. Meta is where we tell them. Visual before-and-afters, rental-income calculators, "a suite for your parents" creative, targeted at homeowners 40-plus with the four motivations. Home-improvement leads here typically run $25-$75.13

Budget to start: $50-80/day, one campaign, four ad sets mapped to the four buyers, creative refreshed off a quarterly factory + site shoot day.

3. Realtor & broker referrals

Borrow trust

Agents stand at the exact "your lot qualifies for a suite" moment. Established ADU builders pay up to $10,000 per closed agent referral.14 We co-brand a "how to finance your backyard suite" kit and turn agents and mortgage brokers into a standing referral engine. Lowest cost per deal in the whole plan.
